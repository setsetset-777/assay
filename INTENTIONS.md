# Assay - Intention notes

---

## Goal

Allow agencies and service providers to clarify client needs early, align on a shared understanding, and formalize project specifications before execution begins.

The tool aims to reduce ambiguity, accelerate project kickoff, and create a clear agreement that both parties can rely on throughout the project.

## Origin

One of the keys to a successful collaboration with a client is the ability to:
	•	help them express their needs clearly
	•	translate those needs into structured, actionable specifications
	•	ensure mutual understanding and agreement before work starts

In practice, this phase is often inefficient:
	•	clients arrive unprepared to kickoff meetings
	•	expectations are vague or incomplete
	•	important constraints are discovered too late
	•	specifications are written after decisions are already misunderstood

This leads to:
	•	wasted time in meetings
	•	misalignment between client and provider
	•	costly revisions and conflicts later

This tool addresses that by introducing a structured, guided, and asynchronous preparation phase, allowing clients to think through their project and communicate their needs and desire clearly.

It ensures that:
	•	agencies receive clear, relevant inputs
	•	both parties align early on expectations and scope
	•	the project starts on a shared and explicit understanding

## Domain

The tool is intentionally generic and designed for any context where **someone builds something on behalf of a client and needs to define expectations clearly**.

It is particularly relevant for:
	•	communication and creative agencies
	•	freelance developers and designers
	•	consulting firms
	•	architects and interior designers
	•	studios and service providers

## Process

### 1. Gather client input

Collect raw input from the client through guided, domain-aware interactions.

The goal is not just to collect answers, but to help the client think about their project.

This step is asynchronous and acts as a preparation phase before meetings.

Supports can include:
	•	structured questionnaires
	•	guided conversational flows
	•	reference materials and examples
	•	workshops or interviews

The system should:
	•	prompt the client on aspects they might overlook
	•	encourage reflection on goals, constraints, and priorities
	•	capture input without prematurely structuring or filtering it

### 2. Analyze input

Once input is gathered, it is reviewed and synthesized to:
	•	identify key themes and priorities
	•	highlight constraints and requirements
	•	detect ambiguities or missing information
	•	surface potential misunderstandings

This step can be:
	•	manual (by the agency)
	•	assisted or automated (through the tool)

A key objective is to transform raw input into structured understanding, while making uncertainties explicit.

### 3. Write specifications

Translate the analyzed input into a structured specification.

This is not a blank document, but an organized representation of the project, including:
	•	objectives
	•	scope
	•	functional requirements
	•	non-functional requirements (timeline, quality, accessibility, etc.)
	•	constraints and assumptions

The specification emerges from the previous steps and reflects a shared interpretation of the project.

### 4. Iterate via feedback loop with client

The goal is to negotiate and refine the understanding, not just present a document.

Both parties can:
	•	comment
	•	suggest edits
	•	ask for clarifications
	•	challenge assumptions

The tool should support:
	•	requirement-level discussions
	•	versioning and history
	•	clear visibility of changes

If a party is not satisfied, they can request a revision, triggering another iteration.

This step ensures that:

the specification becomes a mutually agreed representation of the project, not a unilateral document.

### 5. Validate specifications

Once both parties are aligned, the specification is:
	•	formally approved
	•	timestamped
	•	optionally digitally signed

This creates a clear agreement on what will be delivered.

The validated specification becomes:
	•	the reference for the design and execution phases
	•	a protection against misunderstandings and scope disputes

Clients may still request changes later, but this triggers a new iteration cycle, making scope evolution explicit.

6. (Optional) Track specification fulfillment

After validation, the specification can be used to track fulfillment during execution.

Each requirement becomes a checkpoint:
	•	implemented
	•	partially implemented
	•	revised / renegotiated

Teams can attach evidence (deliverables, links, screenshots) to demonstrate completion.

This enables:
	•	transparency during delivery
	•	alignment with the agreed scope
	•	a final compliance view of the project

At the end, both parties can see:

what was agreed vs what was delivered.

---

## Success Conditions

For the product to succeed, it must deliver clear and immediate value in the early stages of client projects.

1. Faster and better project kickoff

Clients arrive at initial meetings more prepared, with clearer ideas, constraints, and expectations.
Kickoff discussions become more focused and efficient.

2. Reduced ambiguity and misunderstandings

The tool helps surface vague, incomplete, or conflicting inputs early, leading to a clearer shared understanding of the project.

3. Strong perceived value for agencies

Agencies and service providers must feel that the tool:
	•	saves time in discovery and scoping
	•	improves professionalism in client interactions
	•	reduces risk of scope creep and disputes

4. Low friction for clients

Clients can complete the preparation phase easily, without needing to learn a complex tool or adopt new workflows.

5. Repeatable usage across projects

Agencies reuse the tool for multiple client projects, integrating it as a standard step in their project kickoff process.

6. Clear link between agreement and delivery

The specification produced is trusted as a reference throughout the project and, if used, allows teams to demonstrate that agreed requirements have been fulfilled.
